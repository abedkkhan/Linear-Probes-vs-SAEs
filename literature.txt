
**1. Using pretrained SAEs on a frozen LLM**

The foundational interpretability work establishing that SAEs decompose LLM activations into monosemantic features:

- **Bricken et al. (2023)**, *"Towards Monosemanticity: Decomposing Language Models With Dictionary Learning."* 
Anthropic, Transformer Circuits Thread. — Established the SAE methodology for language-model interpretability.
- **Cunningham et al. (2023)**, *"Sparse Autoencoders Find Highly Interpretable Features in Language Models."* — 
Showed SAEs can be applied post-hoc to a frozen LM to find interpretable directions.
- **Templeton et al. (2024)**, *"Scaling Monosemanticity: Extracting Interpretable Features from Claude 3 Sonnet."*
 Anthropic. — Demonstrated SAE-based interpretation at scale.

For our specific SAE (Gemma Scope):

- **Lieberum et al. (2024)**, *"Gemma Scope: Open Sparse Autoencoders Everywhere All At Once on Gemma 2."*
 Google DeepMind. — The release paper for the SAEs we're using.
- **Rajamanoharan et al. (2024)**, *"Jumping Ahead: Improving Reconstruction Fidelity with JumpReLU Sparse 
Autoencoders."* DeepMind. — Describes the JumpReLU architecture used by Gemma Scope.

---

**2. Encoding LLM activations through an SAE for downstream analysis**

- **Gao et al. (2024)**, *"Scaling and evaluating sparse autoencoders."* OpenAI. — 
Lays out the encode/analyse pipeline for SAEs, including the "neighborhood sparse probing" methodology.
- **Marks et al. (2024)**, *"Sparse Feature Circuits: Discovering and Editing Interpretable Causal 
Graphs in Language Models."* — Encodes per-token activations through SAEs to find task-relevant features. 
Directly relevant to our methodology.

---

**3. Dropping the BOS token (attention-sink treatment)**

The attention-sink phenomenon — and the standard practice of excluding BOS from analysis:

- **Xiao et al. (2023)**, *"Efficient Streaming Language Models with Attention Sinks."* ICLR 2024. — 
The paper that named and characterised attention sinks; established that BOS accumulates disproportionate 
attention mass.
- **Cancedda (2024)**, *"Spectral Filters, Dark Signals, and Attention Sinks."* — Documents anomalous BOS 
activation magnitudes and their effect on probing.
- Discussion of BOS as an SAE outlier is also documented in the **Anthropic Transformer Circuits monthly updates 
(2024)** and the **SAELens project documentation/tutorials** (Bloom et al.) — where dropping BOS before SAE 
analysis is recommended as standard practice.

---

**4. Pooling per-token SAE features into a sentence-level representation**

- **Marks et al. (2024)** (above) uses both mean and max aggregation across tokens to derive sentence-level SAE 
features.
- **Kantamneni & Tegmark (2025)**, *"Are Sparse Autoencoders Useful? A Case Study in Sparse Probing."* — 
Systematically compares mean-pool vs max-pool of SAE features for downstream classification. Most directly 
relevant to our pooling choice.
- For the general technique of mean/max pooling token-level representations into sentence vectors: 
**Reimers & Gurevych (2019)**, *"Sentence-BERT,"* EMNLP. — Long-established baseline in NLP.

---

**5. Selecting one SAE feature by correlation with labels (sparse probing)**

This exact methodology — score every SAE feature against a label vector, take the top one — 
is called **sparse probing** in the literature:

- **Kantamneni & Tegmark (2025)** (above) — The definitive paper on sparse probing with SAEs. 
Uses Pearson correlation between SAE feature columns and binary labels to rank features. 
Our Step 5d follows this protocol almost exactly.
- **Gao et al. (2024)** (above) — Introduces "1-sparse probing" (single SAE feature classifier) as an 
SAE evaluation metric.
- **Gurnee et al. (2023)**, *"Finding Neurons in a Haystack: Case Studies with Sparse Probing."* — 
Earlier work that established sparse probing (on raw neurons before SAEs); the conceptual ancestor.
- **Bricken et al. (2023)** (above) — Original feature-discovery-by-label-activation work in the SAE setting.
