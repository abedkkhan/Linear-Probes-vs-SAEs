We are testing the Linear Representation Hypothesis for sentiment in Gemma 2 2B's layer 12.
The linear probe finds the single direction in the 2304-dim residual stream that best separates positive from negative reviews —
its weight vector is the candidate sentiment direction. If this direction achieves high accuracy on held-out test data, it confirms
that sentiment is represented linearly at this layer. The SAE will independently produce its own candidate sentiment direction without
seeing any labels, and the cosine similarity between the two will tell us how much the methods agree.

1. THE DATA — answered in plain language (before preprocessing)
================================================================================

Q: Which dataset did we pick, and from where?

We picked the Stanford Sentiment Treebank version 2, almost always just called
SST-2. It is one of the most widely used datasets in natural language
processing — it has been a standard benchmark for sentiment-classification
work for over a decade. We downloaded it from the Hugging Face hub under the
identifier `stanfordnlp/sst2`, which is the official Stanford NLP group's
distribution of it. Hugging Face is essentially a public library where
researchers host datasets and models, so anyone running our code can pull the
same exact version of the data automatically.

Q: What is the data actually about?

It is a collection of short snippets taken from movie reviews originally
published on the Rotten Tomatoes website. Each snippet was labelled by human
annotators as either positive (label 1) or negative (label 0), and that's the
entire dataset — just text and a binary label. The full SST-2 contains roughly
67,000 training examples, 872 validation examples, and 1,821 test examples,
although for our small initial experiment we only use a slice of those.
Importantly, we do not generate this data ourselves; it is a pre-existing,
well-studied benchmark, which makes our results comparable to other published
work and removes any risk of accidental bias from us creating examples by hand.

Q: What is the nature of the text?

This is the part most people don't realise about SST-2 until they actually
look at it. The dataset is built from the Stanford Sentiment Treebank, where
each full sentence from a movie review was parsed into a tree of phrases, and
every single phrase along that tree was labelled with a sentiment by human
annotators. So the data contains a strange mixture — some entries are full
sentences like "that loves its characters and communicates something rather
beautiful about human nature", and others are tiny fragments like "pathetic
junk", "bring tissues", "genial-rogue shtick", or even just "irritates and".
Lengths range from two or three words up to a few dozen. The vocabulary is
movie-criticism English, sometimes formal and literary, sometimes very
colloquial. Roughly 56% of the examples are labelled positive and 44%
negative, so the dataset is almost balanced but slightly tilted toward
positive.

Q: Why this dataset for our project?

Sentiment is one of the simplest and most well-studied "concepts" in NLP. If
we want to test whether a language model represents abstract concepts as
linear directions inside its activations, sentiment is the ideal first test
case: the labels are clean, the dataset is small enough to iterate quickly,
and any researcher can reproduce the experiment without paying for compute or
licensing data. It is, essentially, the hello-world of probing experiments —
a controlled environment where we can debug our methodology before applying
it to harder concepts later.

2. STAGE 1 PRE-PROCESSING — turning raw SST-2 into our working set
================================================================================

The first thing we did was prepare the text itself, in
scripts/01_prepare_dataset.py. The raw SST-2 download contains 67,349 training
examples, 872 validation examples, and 1,821 test examples — far too many for
a fast initial experiment. So we did three things.

First, we dropped the official "test" split entirely. SST-2's test split has
its labels deliberately hidden (every label is set to -1) because Stanford
keeps it secret as a leaderboard. We can't evaluate on it, so we ignored it.

Second, we carved out our own subsets. We kept only 2000 examples for
training, 500 for validation, and 500 for our own test. The 2000 train + 500
test were both sampled from the original training split, while our 500
validation came from the original validation split. We picked these examples
randomly but with a fixed random seed (42), so the same exact 3000 examples
are selected every time anyone runs the script — that's how we make the
experiment reproducible.

Third, we saved them to disk in a clean format. Each subset went into a JSON
file (data/train_samples.json and so on) containing just the sentence text
and its label, plus a manifest file recording which seed and which source
indices we used. We did NOT tokenise yet, did NOT clean punctuation, did NOT
lowercase, did NOT remove stopwords — the text is kept exactly as Stanford
published it. The reason we don't do classical NLP preprocessing is that
Gemma's tokenizer is the only thing that needs to "understand" the text, and
it expects raw, unmodified strings.

================================================================================
3. THE MODEL — which model, why, and what kind of model
================================================================================

Q: Which model did we pick?

We picked Gemma 2 2B, Google DeepMind's small open-weights language model
from the Gemma 2 family. We downloaded it from Hugging Face under the
identifier `google/gemma-2-2b`. The "2B" in the name means it has roughly
2 billion parameters — small by today's standards (compared to GPT-4 or
Llama 70B), but still a serious, capable model.

Q: Why this specific model?

There is one decisive reason and a few supporting reasons.

The decisive reason is that Google has released pretrained Sparse
Autoencoders for Gemma 2, under a project called Gemma Scope. SAEs are
difficult and expensive to train from scratch — it can take days on big GPUs.
Because Gemma Scope already provides ready-made SAEs trained on every layer
of Gemma 2 2B, we can simply download them and skip training entirely. That
single fact dictated the model choice: we needed a model that has open SAEs
available, and Gemma 2 2B is one of the very few that does. Picking any other
model would have meant either training our own SAEs (slow and risky for an
MVP) or abandoning the SAE comparison altogether.

The supporting reasons are practical. Gemma 2 2B is small enough to fit
comfortably on consumer hardware — about 5 GB in half precision — so it runs
on the M4 MacBook with no need for cloud GPUs. It is fully open-weights,
meaning anyone can reproduce the experiment for free. And it's well
documented and well studied, so if anything goes wrong we can find help.

Q: What kind of model is it?

Gemma 2 2B is a decoder-only Transformer language model, the same broad
family as GPT, Llama, and Mistral. In simple terms, that means it was trained
to do one thing: read a piece of text and predict the next token. By doing
this on enormous amounts of internet text during pretraining, it has
internally learned grammar, factual knowledge, reasoning patterns, and —
relevantly for us — high-level semantic features like sentiment, even though
no one ever explicitly told it about sentiment.

The architecture has 26 stacked decoder layers. Each layer reads from a
shared "scratchpad" called the residual stream, performs some computation
(attention plus a feed-forward block), and writes its result back to the
scratchpad. The scratchpad at every layer carries 2304 numbers per token —
that 2304 is the number we keep mentioning, because it's the dimension of
every activation vector we extract.

Q: Why "frozen"?

We never train or fine-tune Gemma. We only run it forward to look at what it
has already learned during its pretraining. The whole point of the experiment
is to study representations that emerged naturally from next-token
prediction, not representations we deliberately tuned for sentiment. So
Gemma stays exactly as Google released it — we just listen to its layer 12
and read out the activations.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3a. Inside the architecture — decoder layers, residual stream, and 2304-dim
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Gemma 2 2B is a decoder-only Transformer with 26 stacked decoder layers.
Every layer operates on the same shared object — the residual stream — which
is a tensor of shape (sequence_length, 2304). Each token in your input has
its own 2304-dimensional vector inside this stream, and that vector gets
progressively refined as it passes through the 26 layers.

What the residual stream actually is

The residual stream is the central communication channel of the model.
Think of it as a shared workspace that every layer reads from and writes to.
When you tokenise a sentence, the embedding layer initialises one 2304-dim
vector per token — these vectors are essentially "what does this token mean
in isolation". Then layer 1 reads this stream, computes some transformation,
and ADDS its output back into the stream. Layer 2 does the same. And so on
for all 26 layers.

The crucial property is additivity: layers don't replace what's there, they
accumulate. Mathematically, if x_i is the residual stream entering layer i:

    x_{i+1} = x_i + Layer_i(x_i)

This is why it's called residual — every layer's output is added on top of
the existing stream. A consequence is that information written into the
stream by an early layer is still accessible at every subsequent layer
(unless later layers actively cancel it out).

What each decoder layer computes

A Gemma 2 decoder layer has two sub-modules, applied in sequence with
residual connections around each:

1. Multi-head self-attention. This is the mechanism that lets every token
   "see" every preceding token in the sequence. Each head computes weighted
   averages of other tokens' representations and writes the result back
   into the residual stream. This is how the model builds up contextual
   meaning — the representation of "off" in "off his game" becomes different
   from "off" in "off the shelf", because attention pulled in information
   from the surrounding tokens.

2. Feed-forward block (MLP). A two-layer fully-connected network applied
   independently to each token's residual-stream vector. This is where the
   bulk of the model's parameters live and where most of the actual
   computation happens — extracting non-linear features, recombining
   concepts, performing what we'd loosely call "reasoning" on each token's
   representation.

In Gemma 2 these sub-modules also have RMSNorm normalisation applied before
each, which is a Gemma-family detail — important if you're doing low-level
interpretability but not central to the conceptual picture.

Why 2304 specifically

2304 is Gemma 2 2B's hidden size (d_model). It is the dimensionality of the
residual stream and is fixed throughout the entire model — every layer
reads, transforms, and writes 2304-dimensional vectors per token. This is a
hyperparameter chosen by Google when designing the model. It is why every
activation vector we extract is exactly 2304 long, and why the linear probe
has exactly 2304 weights.

Why this matters for our experiment

Two architectural facts justify our methodology:

1. The residual stream is the model's main bus. If a high-level concept like
   sentiment is being computed and used by the model, it almost has to be
   written into the residual stream at some point — that's the only channel
   through which information flows between layers. So hooking the residual
   stream is the natural place to look for emergent concepts.

2. Layer 12 is the midpoint of the 26-layer stack. Empirical work on decoder
   LLMs (Alain & Bengio 2017; Park et al. 2024; the Gemma Scope paper)
   consistently finds that semantic, task-level concepts emerge in the
   middle layers — early layers handle tokenisation and syntax, late layers
   compress everything down for next-token prediction, and middle layers
   carry the richest abstract representations. Layer 12 sits squarely in
   that middle zone.

That's why we hook the residual stream output of layer 12: it's the model's
shared workspace at the point in the computation where abstract concepts
like sentiment are most likely to be cleanly represented.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3b. What if we hooked a different layer — e.g. layer 20?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Layer 20 is in the late zone of Gemma 2 2B — five layers from the end.
Hooking there changes what kind of representation you're reading.

What layer 20 has that layer 12 doesn't

By the time the residual stream reaches layer 20, eight more decoder layers
have written into it on top of whatever was there at layer 12. Some of that
extra computation almost certainly refines sentiment further — there's
evidence in the Gemma Scope paper that some sentiment-related features
remain present and even sharpen in later layers. So a probe at layer 20
might still work very well — possibly in the high 80s or low 90s, even
matching layer 12.

What it loses, and why

The reason late layers are usually worse for probing — even though they
have "more computation" behind them — is a subtle but important point about
what the model is actually using the residual stream for in late layers.

In a decoder-only Transformer, the residual stream's job changes as you go
deeper. In early layers it accumulates linguistic and conceptual structure.
In middle layers it carries the richest, most general, most abstract
semantic representations — which is why probes work best there. But in the
last several layers, the residual stream begins to be re-purposed. The
model is now preparing to make a single concrete prediction: the next
token. So those late layers start projecting the rich middle-layer
representation back down toward something that looks like the model's
vocabulary — directions in residual space increasingly correspond to "this
token is likely next" rather than "this is a sentiment-bearing concept".

Concretely, this means three things tend to happen by layer 20:

1. Sentiment becomes less cleanly linear. It hasn't disappeared, but it
   gets entangled with token-prediction information, so a single linear
   direction can no longer separate the classes as crisply.

2. The representation becomes more token-specific. Late layers care a lot
   about which exact word is about to come next. So sentences ending in
   different words may have systematically different residual streams even
   if their sentiment is the same — adding noise from the probe's
   perspective.

3. The probe's accuracy may stay high but the probe's direction becomes
   less interpretable. A probe at layer 20 might still classify well, but
   its weight vector is now a hybrid of "sentiment" and "next-token
   vocabulary structure", which makes it less useful as a clean answer to
   "what does sentiment look like in Gemma".

What the published evidence actually shows

For Gemma 2 2B specifically, the Gemma Scope paper reports residual-stream
probing curves that look like an inverted-U — accuracy rising sharply from
layer 0, peaking somewhere in the middle, and then drifting gently downward
in the late layers. They never crash to baseline; they just stop improving
and slowly fade. So a layer-20 probe would likely give us competitive but
slightly lower accuracy than layer 12, with a less interpretable direction.

And critically: the SAE comparison

There is an additional reason layer choice matters for our specific
project. Gemma Scope publishes pretrained SAEs at every layer, but each SAE
is trained on the activations of one specific layer. Whichever layer we
pick for the linear probe, we have to use the SAE trained on the same
layer for the comparison to be valid. So choosing a different layer doesn't
just change the probe — it also swaps in a different pretrained SAE, and
the quality and interpretability of that SAE's discovered features may be
different. The Gemma Scope team has reported that mid-layer SAEs tend to
find the most clearly-interpretable, high-level features (sentiment, time,
sentiment polarity, named entities, etc.), while late-layer SAEs find more
token-prediction-flavoured features.

Bottom line

A probe at layer 20 would probably still work — perhaps in the high 80s —
but with slightly lower accuracy than layer 12, a less interpretable probe
direction (because it gets mixed with next-token-prediction information),
and a less interpretable SAE to compare against.

Plan: we will sweep one layer after another from 0 through 25 and train a
probe at each, to test this hypothesis empirically and identify which layer
carries the cleanest sentiment representation in Gemma 2 2B.

================================================================================
4. WHAT IS A HOOK — how we actually listen to layer 12
================================================================================

A hook is a callback function — a small piece of code that gets
automatically executed at a specific point during the model's forward pass,
without changing what the model is doing.

Mechanically, here's what happens. PyTorch represents Gemma as a tree of
Module objects — the top-level model contains 26 decoder-layer modules, each
of which contains attention and MLP sub-modules, and so on. Whenever you
call model(inputs), PyTorch walks through this tree and runs each module's
forward() method in order, passing the output of one module as the input to
the next.

PyTorch lets you register a hook on any module. A hook is a function you
give PyTorch in advance that says: "every time this specific module finishes
running its forward pass, call my function with the inputs the module
received and the output it produced." PyTorch keeps a list of registered
hooks for every module, and after each module's forward() returns, it calls
every hook in that list before moving on to the next module.

In our case, we register a hook on model.model.layers[12] — the 13th
decoder layer (zero-indexed). After that layer finishes computing, PyTorch
hands our hook function the layer's output. Our hook just grabs that
output, detaches it from the computation graph (so we don't accidentally
compute gradients we don't need), and stores it in a Python dictionary.
Then PyTorch carries on running layers 13, 14, ..., 25 as if nothing
happened. The model still produces its normal next-token prediction at the
end. We just also have a copy of layer 12's output sitting in our
dictionary, ready to use.

Why this is the right tool for the job

Three properties matter for our experiment:

1. It is non-invasive. The hook does not modify the residual stream,
   doesn't change the model's behaviour, doesn't touch the weights. The
   model runs exactly as it would without our hook attached. We are reading,
   not editing.

2. It runs at exactly the right moment. We don't want layer 11's output,
   we don't want layer 13's, we want layer 12's output. By registering the
   hook on the specific module model.model.layers[12], PyTorch guarantees we
   capture the residual stream at exactly that point in the computation.

3. It captures the tensor we want, not something downstream. PyTorch passes
   our hook the direct output of Gemma2DecoderLayer.forward(). For Gemma
   decoder layers this is a tuple, and the first element of that tuple is
   the residual stream — i.e. the output x_{12} + Layer_12(x_{12}) from the
   additivity formula in section 3a. So we are reading the residual stream
   at the boundary between layer 12 and layer 13, which is precisely what
   we wanted.

What our hook function looks like in plain English

    def hook(module, inputs, output):
        captured["hidden"] = output[0].detach()

That's it. Three lines: ignore `module` and `inputs`; take the first
element of the output tuple (the residual stream); call .detach() to strip
out gradient tracking; store it in a dictionary called `captured` that
lives in the outer scope.

Then we register it once with
model.model.layers[12].register_forward_hook(hook), and from then on every
forward pass through Gemma silently fills captured["hidden"] with the
layer-12 residual stream. After we're done, we call handle.remove() to
detach the hook and the model goes back to behaving normally.

Why hooks instead of editing the model code

We could in principle have copied Gemma's source code, edited the forward
function to return layer 12's output, and run that modified model. But
that's brittle, error-prone, and ties our code to a specific version of
Hugging Face Transformers. Hooks are the standard, clean,
framework-supported way to extract internal activations from any PyTorch
model — and crucially, they let us inspect the original, unmodified model,
which is essential for an interpretability study.

5. RUNNING THE EXTRACTION — sub-questions

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Q1. How did we initiate the experiment?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The extraction happens in scripts/02_extract_activations.py. The setup,
before any sentences are processed, has four parts.

Authentication. We load the Hugging Face token from .env and call login().
This is necessary because Gemma 2 is a gated model — Google requires every
download to be authenticated against a user account that has accepted the
license. Without this step, the model download would fail with a 401 error.

Device selection. We check whether MPS (Apple's GPU backend on M-series
chips) is available, and if so we set device = torch.device("mps"). This
means the model and every tensor we feed it will live on the GPU's unified
memory, and matrix multiplications will run on the GPU rather than the CPU.
On the M4 this is roughly an order of magnitude faster than CPU for
transformer inference.

Loading the model and tokenizer. We call
AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained("google/gemma-2-2b") to get Gemma's exact
tokenizer (the same SentencePiece vocabulary the model was trained with),
and AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained("google/gemma-2-2b",
dtype=torch.float16).to(device) to load the model in half precision and
move it to the GPU. We then call model.eval() to put the model into
evaluation mode — this disables dropout and other training-only behaviour,
which matters because we want every forward pass to be deterministic. We do
this once at the start of the script and reuse the same model for all
three splits, rather than reloading the 10 GB of weights three times.

Registering the hook. We define our small hook function and attach it to
model.model.layers[12] with register_forward_hook(...). From this moment
on, every time the model runs a forward pass, layer 12's output is silently
copied into a Python dictionary called `captured`, which we read after each
pass. We register the hook once before the loop, not once per sentence —
there's no need to attach and detach it 3000 times.

After all four steps, we are ready to start feeding sentences. Note one
important detail: the loop runs inside a torch.no_grad() context. This
tells PyTorch not to build a computation graph for backpropagation, since
we will never need gradients here. It saves both memory and time — roughly
cuts memory usage in half on this kind of forward-only workload.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Q2. What did the hook actually extract?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Once the loop starts, every iteration does the same thing for one sentence.
Here is what happens at the moment the hook fires.

We tokenise the sentence with
tokenizer(row["sentence"], return_tensors="pt").to(device). This produces a
small dictionary containing two tensors — input_ids of shape (1, T) where
T is the number of tokens (including the prepended <bos>), and
attention_mask of the same shape, both sitting on the MPS GPU. We then
call model(**inputs). PyTorch begins walking through Gemma's 26 decoder
layers in order, and when layer 12 finishes computing, our hook function
is called.

The hook receives three arguments — the module itself, the inputs that
went into it, and the output it produced — and we only care about the
third. For a Gemma2DecoderLayer, that output is a Python tuple, by Hugging
Face convention. The first element of the tuple is the residual stream
after layer 12 has added its contribution. Other elements (when present)
are things like attention weights or a key-value cache for autoregressive
generation, but we don't need those.

So we take output[0]. This is a tensor of shape (1, T, 2304). Reading the
dimensions: 1 because we're processing one sentence at a time (the batch
dimension); T because layer 12 produces one residual-stream vector for
every token in the sentence (so a 6-token sentence gives 6 vectors, an
11-token sentence gives 11); and 2304 because that's Gemma 2 2B's hidden
size — every residual-stream vector is 2304 numbers long, fixed by the
model's architecture.

The dtype of this tensor is torch.float16, because the entire model is
running in fp16. The device is mps, because the tensor lives on the GPU.

We immediately call .detach() on this tensor before storing it. This is
important because PyTorch by default tracks every tensor's history of
operations to allow backpropagation, and even under torch.no_grad() it can
still hold references that prevent memory from being freed. .detach()
returns a copy of the tensor that is decoupled from the autograd graph
entirely, so once we're done with it, the GPU memory can be reclaimed.

After the hook stores this tensor in captured["hidden"], layer 12 returns
control to PyTorch and Gemma carries on running layers 13 through 25. The
model still produces a final output (the next-token logits), but we ignore
it — our entire purpose has already been served the moment layer 12
finished. We have a copy of the residual stream we wanted, sitting in our
dictionary, ready for the next step.

To answer the question precisely: the hook extracts a (1, T, 2304)
half-precision tensor on GPU, representing the residual stream output of
Gemma 2 2B's layer 12, with one 2304-dimensional vector per token in the
input sentence.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Q3. My confusions about labels, shapes, and mean-pooling — clarified
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: How does the label of a sentence get matched to its activation?
A: By position. At iteration i: pooled[i] = activation, labels[i] = label.
   Same i for both. Two parallel arrays, written together.

Q: Is this label-matching like a sub-process running during the forward pass?
A: No. Just two ordinary lines that run right after the forward pass, in
   the same loop iteration. Sequential.

Q: pooled[i] is the activation vector we extracted, right?
A: Yes — the 2304-dim mean-pooled vector for sentence i.

Q: Before running the forward pass, do we already know the label?
A: Yes. Labels come from the SST-2 JSON file. We just carry them along.

Q: So we already have the labels, and during the forward pass we take the
   mean of T column-wise?
A: Yes.

Q: Can you give me a tiny example? I'm confused about where the average
   value goes.
A: Suppose hidden = 4 and T = 3. The (T, 2304) matrix becomes:

       token 0:  [ 2,  4,  6,  8]
       token 1:  [ 4,  6,  8, 10]
       token 2:  [ 6,  8, 10, 12]

   Average each column:
       (2+4+6)/3=4   (4+6+8)/3=6   (6+8+10)/3=8   (8+10+12)/3=10

   Result: [4, 6, 8, 10]. The 3 rows are gone. Replaced by one row of
   column averages.

Q: But we had (1, T, 2304). What does that look like?
A: A 3-D box.
       axis 0 = batch (size 1)
       axis 1 = tokens (size T)
       axis 2 = hidden dims (size 2304)
   The leading 1 is just a wrapper. Inside it is the real (T, 2304) matrix.

Q: So the 1 is not actually a row or column?
A: Correct. It just means "one sentence at a time". PyTorch always wants
   batched input, even if the batch has one item.

Q: So the actual matrix is (T, 2304), and after mean-pooling T the tensor
   becomes (1, 2304), but we write it as (2304,)?
A: Yes. After .mean(dim=1) it's (1, 2304). After .squeeze(0) the size-1
   wrapper is dropped and it becomes a plain 1-D vector of length 2304.

Q: Where does T go after the mean?
A: It disappears. T vectors were stacked on top of each other; averaging
   collapses them into one vector. T no longer exists as a dimension.

Q: Is mean-pooling the same as normalising?
A: No. Mean-pooling = averaging T vectors into one. Normalising = scaling
   values to a fixed range. Normalising happens later, in StandardScaler,
   right before training the probe.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Q4. How are we sure the input is going into the model correctly?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: How do we know the right tokenizer is being used?
A: We load it from the same model ID as the model itself
   (google/gemma-2-2b). So the sub-word vocabulary, the special tokens,
   and the encoding rules are exactly what Gemma was trained with. If we
   mixed in any other tokenizer, the token IDs would be meaningless to
   the model.

Q: How do we know the input tensors end up in the right place?
A: We call .to(device) on the tokenizer output, so input_ids and
   attention_mask land on MPS — the same place the model lives. If they
   were on CPU and the model on GPU, PyTorch would crash with a clear
   device-mismatch error. No crash means devices line up.

Q: How do we actually verify Gemma is seeing the sentence the way it
   expects?
A: We did a quick sanity check called the "pilot run". We took just one
   sentence — "as a director, eastwood is off his game" — and asked the
   tokenizer to break it into the small pieces (tokens) it was about to
   feed into the model. Then we printed those pieces back in
   human-readable form so we could see exactly what Gemma is going to see.

   The tokenizer split the sentence into eleven pieces:

   ['<bos>', 'as', ' a', ' director', ' ,', ' east', 'wood', ' is',
    ' off', ' his', ' game']

   Three things we wanted to confirm by looking at this list:

   1. The very first piece is <bos> — short for "beginning of sentence".
      Gemma always expects this special marker at the start of any input.
      If it were missing, the model would still run but its activations
      would be slightly off, because the model wasn't trained that way.
      Seeing <bos> here confirms the tokenizer added it correctly.

   2. The word "eastwood" was split into two pieces, east + wood. This is
      normal. Tokenizers don't have a slot for every possible word in the
      language — instead they learn a vocabulary of common chunks, and
      rarer words get assembled out of smaller chunks. The fact that
      "eastwood" is split exactly the way Gemma's vocabulary expects is
      good — it means the tokenizer matches the model.

   3. Most pieces start with a space, like ' a' instead of just 'a'. This
      is also normal for Gemma. Its tokenizer treats the space before a
      word as part of that word's token. So 'a' and ' a' are different
      tokens in Gemma's vocabulary. Seeing the leading spaces preserved
      confirms nothing got accidentally stripped or cleaned up before it
      reached the model.

   So the takeaway is: the sentence we wrote in plain English is being
   fed into Gemma exactly the way Gemma expects to receive English text,
   with the right special markers, the right sub-word splits, and the
   right spacing. No silent corruption.

Q: If I have an activation vector of shape (2304,), can I print the
   original sentence from it?
A: No — pooling is one-way, the original tokens are gone. But you can
   always look up the sentence by index, since we kept everything
   aligned. rows[i]["sentence"], labels[i] and acts[i] all describe the
   same sentence because they were written in the same order in the same
   loop. The JSON file is the human-readable side of the pipeline,
   looked up by the same index i.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Q6. How logistic regression actually works — broken into small bits
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Bit 1 — What logistic regression IS, before any training

It's a tiny model with two ingredients:
  - A weight vector w — same length as the input. For us, length 2304.
  - A bias b — just one number.

Total parameters: 2305.

Given any input vector x, the model produces one number called the logit:
       logit = w · x + b

That w · x is just the dot product: multiply each weight by the matching
input number, sum them all up. The result is a single scalar.

This scalar is the model's "raw answer":
  - Big positive logit → "I think this is class 1"
  - Big negative logit → "I think this is class 0"
  - Around zero → "I'm not sure"

That's the whole machinery.

Bit 2 — Turning the logit into a probability

The logit can be anything from −∞ to +∞. We need to turn it into something
between 0 and 1. We use the sigmoid function:

       probability = 1 / (1 + e^(-logit))

Examples:
  logit = 0  → probability = 0.5  (totally unsure)
  logit = +5 → probability ≈ 0.99 (very confident it's class 1)
  logit = -5 → probability ≈ 0.01 (very confident it's class 0)

The model's prediction: if probability > 0.5, predict positive; otherwise
predict negative. Equivalently: predict positive whenever the logit is
positive.

Bit 3 — How training finds w and b

At the start, w and b are just random numbers. The model knows nothing.
Training is the process of slowly nudging w and b until predictions match
the true labels as well as possible.

Step 1 — Measure how wrong we are
   For each training sentence we compute the predicted probability p and
   compare it to the true label y. We sum up a single number across all
   2000 examples called the loss.
   Loss is small when the model is confident AND right. Loss is large
   when the model is confident AND wrong. Minimising the loss = making
   the model right and confident.
   (Formula: binary cross-entropy. Don't need to memorise it.)

Step 2 — Figure out which way to nudge each weight
   For each of the 2305 parameters, we ask: if I increase this parameter
   slightly, does the loss go up or down?
   The answer is called the gradient. Calculus gives the gradients in
   closed form. sklearn computes them automatically.

Step 3 — Take a step
       w_new = w_old − (small step) × (gradient for w)
       b_new = b_old − (small step) × (gradient for b)
   The "small step" is the learning rate. Too big = overshoot. Too small
   = slow.

Step 4 — Repeat
   Compute new predictions, new loss, new gradients, take another step.
   When gradients become tiny, no direction reduces the loss further —
   the model has converged.

In our run, sklearn converged in 60 iterations. After that, w is the
2304-dim sentiment direction and b is the bias — both saved to disk.

Q7. How we actually used logistic regression in our experiment — small bits
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Bit 1 — Loading the training data

We loaded the activations and labels saved earlier:

    X_train = np.load("data/train_activations.npy")   # shape (2000, 2304)
    y_train = np.load("data/train_labels.npy")        # shape (2000,)

X_train is a matrix: 2000 rows (one per sentence), 2304 columns (the
activation vector for each sentence). y_train is 2000 numbers, each one
0 (negative) or 1 (positive).

The labels are NOT inside the matrix. They live in a separate 1-D array.
But they are aligned by row order: X_train[i] is the activation for the
sentence whose label is y_train[i]. When we later call clf.fit(X, y),
sklearn knows row i of X belongs to label i of y.

We did the same for X_val/y_val and X_test/y_test, but those splits are
held aside — the probe never sees them during training.

Bit 2 — Standardising the features

Before training we rescaled the activations:

    scaler = StandardScaler()
    X_train_s = scaler.fit_transform(X_train)
    X_val_s   = scaler.transform(X_val)
    X_test_s  = scaler.transform(X_test)

For each of the 2304 columns it computes the column's mean and std (across
the 2000 training rows), subtracts the mean (column centred at 0), and
divides by the std (spread ≈ 1).

Why: each of the 2304 dimensions is a different learned channel inside
Gemma. Across the 2000 training sentences, some columns naturally produce
big values (e.g. ±40) and others tiny ones (e.g. ±0.01). Without scaling,
the optimiser would obsess over the big-valued dims and L2 regularisation
wouldn't penalise weights fairly. Standardising puts every column on the
same footing.

Tiny example. Pretend each sentence gives just 3 numbers:

    sentence  dim 0   dim 1    dim 2
       s1       30    0.02      -1
       s2      -25   -0.01       2
       s3       40    0.03      -3
       s4      -35   -0.02       1

Looking down each column: dim 0 is naturally big, dim 1 is naturally tiny,
dim 2 is medium. "Big" and "small" doesn't mean within one sentence — it
means column-wise across many sentences.

Important: fit_transform only on train; transform (no fit) on val/test.
That way val/test scaling uses train statistics — no data leakage.

Bit 3 — Creating and fitting the probe

    clf = LogisticRegression(
        C=1.0,
        max_iter=1000,
        random_state=42,
        penalty="l2",
        solver="lbfgs",
    )
    clf.fit(X_train_s, y_train)

LogisticRegression(...) creates an empty model — w and b don't exist yet.
C=1.0 is the strength of L2 regularisation. max_iter=1000 limits the
optimiser to 1000 steps. random_state=42 fixes randomness. solver="lbfgs"
is the gradient-based optimisation algorithm.

clf.fit(X_train_s, y_train) runs the full optimisation from Q6 Bit 3:
start with random w and b; compute loss across all 2000 sentences; nudge
w and b to reduce the loss; repeat until convergence.

Our run converged in 60 iterations (well below the 1000 limit).

After fit() finishes, clf holds:
  - clf.coef_[0]      → trained weight vector w of shape (2304,)
  - clf.intercept_[0] → trained bias b

That w is our sentiment direction.

Bit 4 — Evaluating on val and test

    pred  = clf.predict(X_val_s)
    proba = clf.predict_proba(X_val_s)[:, 1]

clf.predict returns 0 or 1 for each of the 500 val sentences (internally:
compute w·x + b, sigmoid it, threshold at 0.5). clf.predict_proba returns
the probability of being positive.

We computed accuracy, F1, AUC, and the confusion matrix on val and test.

Results:
    split   accuracy   f1       auc
    train   1.0000     1.0000   1.0000
    val     0.9140     0.9152   0.9712
    test    0.9200     0.9216   0.9728

Train is 100% — the probe memorised training data. Val and test ~92% —
that's the real number, since the probe never saw those sentences.

Bit 5 — Saving the artefacts

    pickle.dump(clf,    open("models/linear_probe.pkl", "wb"))
    pickle.dump(scaler, open("models/scaler.pkl",       "wb"))
    np.save("results/probe_direction.npy", clf.coef_[0].astype(np.float32))
    json.dump(metrics, open("results/probe_metrics.json", "w"), indent=2)

What each file contains:

  - linear_probe.pkl   → the trained sklearn classifier object (the
                         learned w of shape (2304,), the bias b, and
                         sklearn's internal config). Reusable as-is to
                         predict on any new activation.
  - scaler.pkl         → the fitted StandardScaler holding the per-column
                         means and stds from the training set, so we can
                         rescale any new activation the same way.
  - probe_direction.npy → just the raw weight vector w saved as a NumPy
                          array of shape (2304,) — the sentiment direction
                          on its own (this is the artefact we'll later
                          cosine-similarity against the SAE).
  - probe_metrics.json → a plain JSON file with accuracy, F1, AUC, and
                         the confusion matrix for train/val/test.

Why we need scaler.pkl alongside the probe: the probe was trained on
standardised activations. To predict on a new sentence we must (1) run
forward pass to get raw activation x, (2) apply the same scaling
x_scaled = (x - scaler.mean_) / scaler.scale_, (3) call clf.predict.
Without the scaler the probe is unusable on new data.

Q5. Quick recap — the three runs
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q: We had 3000 sentences total with positive and negative labels. We only
   gave those sentences to the model — 2000 separate, 500 separate, and
   500 separate — and saved their activations in three separate files,
   right?
A: Yes. Three separate runs through Gemma, three separate sets of
   activation + label files in data/:
       train_*.npy  — 2000 sentences
       val_*.npy    —  500 sentences
       test_*.npy   —  500 sentences
   The model sees them as independent batches; the only reason we keep
   them apart is so we can later use train for fitting the probe, val
   for tuning, and test as the sealed final exam. Same model, same hook,
   same procedure — just three separate input lists and three separate
   output files.
